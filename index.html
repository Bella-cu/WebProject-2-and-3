Welcome to our Treaty One website! Treaty One was the first treaty made in what is now Manitoba. It was an important agreement between the Canadian government and Indigenous Peoples. This website will help you learn about where it happened, the communities involved, and why it still matters today.

History

Treaty One was an agreement made on August 3, 1871, between the First Nations people and the Canadian government. At first, it seemed like a good deal as the government promised the First Nation people food, supplies, and land. They also promised each family of five 160 acres to live and farm on. But soon after the treaty was signed, the Indigenous people realized they didn’t get everything they were promised. They told the Canadian government, but their voices went unheard as settlers kept moving onto their land anyway. Even today, the broken promises from Treaty One are still talked about as people are still trying to fix the mistakes that were made so long ago. Source

Negotiations

Treaty One was negotiated in 1871 at Lower Fort Garry, located along the Red River in Manitoba. The meeting brought together leaders from the First Nations and government representatives, including Alexander Morris, the Canadian government’s representative. They discussed sharing the land and what the government would provide in return for the land. Source

Communities

Treaty One is connected to the Cree, Saulteaux, and Assiniboine communities because it was an agreement with the Canadian government about sharing their land. These communities lived closely with nature, using the land for food, clothing, and shelter. After the treaty was signed, the government took control of a big area of the land, leaving the First Nations with less space and fewer resources. This change forced them to adapt to a new way of life, and they had to find new ways to live with the less land and fewer resources they had. Source
